Design Process

A structured, research-first approach.

Every design decision traces back to something a user said, felt, or struggled with. This case study follows a double-diamond process — diverging to understand the problem, converging to define it, then repeating through design and validation.

Problem

Students are overwhelmed, not just busy.

University students often struggle to manage multiple assignments, deadlines, and academic responsibilities across different courses. As coursework accumulates, students must keep track of tasks across several classes, each with its own schedule and expectations.

Research in goal-setting theory suggests that large or ambiguous goals can reduce motivation and make it harder for individuals to initiate tasks, while breaking goals into smaller and more specific steps can significantly improve motivation and task completion (Locke & Latham, 2002).

Although many productivity tools exist — Notion, Todoist, Google Calendar — these tools primarily focus on recording tasks and deadlines rather than helping students approach and progress through their work.

Learning management systems such as Canvas display assignments separately within each course, requiring students to manually combine information across platforms. Students are often left with long lists that feel overwhelming rather than helpful for guiding action.

Key Insight
"The core issue is not simply the number of assignments students face, but how those assignments are structured and presented."
User Research

Listening before designing.

To avoid designing for assumptions, I conducted desk research, literature review, and user empathy mapping before touching wireframes. The primary artifact was a deep empathy map for Marco Guo — a 20-year-old Informatics & CS junior at UW — built from qualitative interviews and behavioral observation.

Design Decisions

Every feature starts with a finding.

Rather than listing features, each design decision below maps directly to a specific user pain point or behavioral insight uncovered in research. This traceability was central to my design process.

🗂

Centralized Dashboard

Finding:Students jump between Canvas, email, and calendars every day.
Decision:Put everything in one place so students can start faster and think less.
✅

Step-by-Step Breakdown

Finding:Big tasks make students avoid starting.
Decision:Break assignments into small steps so it feels easier to begin.
📈

Visible Progress

Finding:Students feel better when they can see progress.
Decision:Show clear progress bars for courses and tasks.
🤖

Contextual AI Guidance

Finding:The most common problem was not knowing where to start.
Decision:Use AI to give quick, course-specific help when students get stuck.
📅

Time-Aware Schedule

Finding:Students said their calendars felt cluttered and hard to prioritize.
Decision:Use a simple daily schedule to plan study time around classes.
🔗

Canvas Integration

Finding:Re-entering Canvas data by hand is frustrating and slow.
Decision:Sync directly with Canvas so data stays accurate automatically.
Core Design Principle
"The role of AI here is not to do the work for students — it's to lower the barrier to starting. Preserving student agency was a non-negotiable constraint throughout the design process."
Key Screens

Designed for how students actually work.

Three core screens shaped directly by user research — from scattered task lists to not knowing where to start.

01 — Home Dashboard
1

Category Filter

Filter by Science, Math, CS, or Engineering to surface the most relevant coursework instantly — no context-switching required.

2

Today's Focus

The most urgent task is always front and center, with a live progress bar and a one-tap Continue Session flow to reduce decision fatigue.

3

Active Courses

All assignments at a glance — real-time status badges (Up Next / Later / Done), due dates, and color-coded course icons across every class.

02 — AI Study Assistant
1

Context-Aware Greeting

The AI already knows your current subject, so you can dive straight into a question without re-explaining your situation every session.

2

Conversational Explanations

Responses use plain language, analogies, and structured breakdowns — designed to guide understanding, not just supply answers.

3

Always-Available Input

The input bar stays anchored at the bottom so quick questions are never more than a tap away, mid-study or mid-task.

03 — Step-by-Step Task View
1

Task Header

Course code, assignment name, professor, and due date — all the context you need without opening Canvas, email, or a syllabus.

2

Step-by-Step Breakdown

Large assignments broken into small, checkable steps. One tap marks a step done and automatically advances to the next — helping you stay on track.

3

Built-in Focus Timer

A Pomodoro-style timer suggests a duration based on the current step, keeping study sessions time-bounded and productive.

Evaluation

Testing with real students.

We conducted informal user testing with six UW undergraduate students who frequently manage multiple assignments. Participants completed realistic tasks and participated in short follow-up interviews.

✓ What Worked
  • ✓ Centralized view made workload easier to understand
  • ✓ Visual progress indicators showed completion at a glance
  • ✓ AI guidance was helpful when starting felt unclear
  • ✓ Step-by-step task breakdown reduced overwhelm
  • ✓ Participants understood the workflow quickly
⚠ Limitations
  • ⚠ Small sample size limits generalizability of findings
  • ⚠ Focused on undergrads — may not transfer to other populations
  • ⚠ AI guidance may generate ethically problematic responses
  • ⚠ Canvas integration was simulated, not fully functional
  • ⚠ Long-term behavior change was not measured

Reflection

What I learned — and what I'd do next.

Design is never finished, only shipped. Looking back at this project with fresh eyes, here's what I'd change, what surprised me, and where I'd take the next iteration.

💡 Key Takeaways

  • The "overwhelm" problem turned out to be as much emotional as informational — this shifted my design from a task manager toward emotional scaffolding. I should have surfaced this insight earlier.
  • Involving participants in ideation (co-design), not just validation, would have uncovered unexpected solutions I wouldn't have reached on my own.
  • The AI feature needs more rigorous ethical consideration around academic integrity — a constraint I'd make explicit from day one in a real product.
  • Designing for mobile-first forced clearer prioritization of what actually matters on each screen.

🔭 Next Iterations

  • Conduct longitudinal testing across a full academic quarter to measure whether behavior change persists beyond the novelty effect.
  • Expand research to include graduate students, international students, and community college learners to test generalizability.
  • Explore collaborative study features — research revealed that peer social dynamics are a strong motivator that the current design ignores.
  • Replace the simulated Canvas integration with a real OAuth flow to test whether real data changes how students interact with the system.
Designer's Note
"The most important design decision I made wasn't about the interface — it was choosing to treat academic overwhelm as an emotional and motivational problem, not just an organizational one. That reframing changed everything that followed."

01 ONBOARDING

Welcome

Full-screen hero with teal gradient establishes brand identity. Feature cards explain core value props, and a single CTA reduces decision paralysis immediately.

DESIGN RATIONALE

The gradient hero communicates energy and focus before any feature explanation — the visual tone sets expectations faster than text could.

02 AUTH

Sign In

Clean sign-in form with email and password fields. Consistent with the app's teal color palette, with a secondary link back to the welcome screen.

DESIGN RATIONALE

Keeping auth minimal prevents intimidation — students should feel welcomed, not interrogated. The single-step flow respects their time.

03 CORE · DASHBOARD

Home

Personalized greeting, streak counter, category filter chips, Today's Focus card, and course cards with real-time progress — all in one scrollable view.

DESIGN RATIONALE

The home screen is the emotional anchor. Progress visibility and streak reinforcement address the core finding that students feel better when advancement is measurable.

04 CORE · PLANNING

Schedule

Google Calendar-style daily view with a week strip navigator, hourly time slots, color-coded course blocks precisely positioned by time, and a real-time indicator line.

DESIGN RATIONALE

Seeing classes visually in time helps students plan study sessions around their actual schedule — addressing the "cluttered calendar" pain point directly.

05 CORE · TASKS

Tasks Overview

All courses in one centralized list with per-course progress bars. Completed courses are automatically marked "Done" when all steps are checked off.

DESIGN RATIONALE

Centralization was the most-requested fix in user research. Students shouldn't need to open Canvas and three other apps to understand their total workload.

06 CORE · TASKS

Task Detail

Step-by-step breakdown of a single course assignment with checkboxes, milestone markers, a progress bar, and a celebration state when all steps are complete.

DESIGN RATIONALE

Breaking big assignments into small, checkable steps is the central hypothesis of StudyFlow — this screen is where that hypothesis is directly tested.

07 CORE · ANALYTICS

Progress

Tabbed view across Week, Courses, and Timeline. Shows study hours, task completion rates, course-level progress bars, and an activity timeline.

DESIGN RATIONALE

Students reported feeling relief when progress was visible. The progress screen makes invisible effort tangible — reinforcing momentum and reducing anxiety.

08 CORE · AI

AI Assistant

Conversational AI chat interface with suggested prompts, quick-reply chips, and contextual responses. Accessible from every screen via the bottom nav.

DESIGN RATIONALE

The AI is positioned as a supportive guide, not an answer machine — framing that preserves student agency while lowering the barrier to starting.

09 SETTINGS

Profile

User profile with Canvas LMS integration (connect/disconnect with live status), quick links to Schedule and Tasks, notification and dark mode toggles, and sign-out.

DESIGN RATIONALE

Canvas integration is the bridge between StudyFlow and real coursework. Making it prominent in settings signals that the app is meant to work alongside existing tools, not replace them.
